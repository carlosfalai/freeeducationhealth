Manifesto — for the self-reliant

You can stock a pantry. You cannot stock a diagnosis.

You already store food, water, and power, because you would rather hold a necessity than hope someone hands it to you on a bad day. Health is almost always the piece that gets left out — and it is the one piece a shelf cannot hold.

The missing piece of the prep

A family can have months of food and still have no idea what to do when a child spikes a fever, a wound turns, or a chronic condition flares and the clinic is closed, far, or gone. Supplies answer "what will we eat?" They do not answer "is this serious, and what do we do right now?" Preparedness without health guidance is a house with a locked door and an open window.

The missing piece is not a stockpile of pills. It is the thinking — the part of care that a careful clinician carries in their head — held in a form your household can actually keep.

Health preparedness you own

A coaching layer that runs on your own machine: it asks the structured questions a clinician would ask, teaches the warning signs that mean seek care now, cross-checks its reasoning across more than one independent AI, and leaves you a portable record your family owns and carries. It helps you think clearly about a health problem and know when it truly needs a clinician — whether or not anything else is working.

Fully local, if you want zero dependence

The standard setup uses capable hosted AI models and costs on the order of a few cents of computing per consultation. But for households that want no outside dependence at all, there is a fully local option: run it with Ollama and two different local models on your own hardware. No internet connection, no account, no outside service — the cross-checking panel still applies, because the two models must still agree or tell you they didn't.

Be honest about the trade: small local models are more limited than the large hosted ones. What does not change is the structure — the intake questions, the red-flag rules, the refusal to speak when the panel is short. The discipline travels with the software, not with the network.

Any copy can seed new ones. The whole system is files on a disk. Copy it to a second machine for your family, hand it to a neighbor, keep one offline. If any copy fails, every other copy keeps working — and can start more.

Prudence, not prophecy

None of this predicts an apocalypse. A first-aid kit is not a belief that you will be hurt; a spare tire is not a belief that you will crash. They are how sensible people stay unbothered by bad days precisely because they are ready for them.

Health you own works the same way. Most of its value lands on a normal Tuesday: a clear head about a fever at 2 a.m., a better-prepared visit to a real clinician, a health history that is not trapped in someone else's system. That it would also still be there on a harder day is a bonus, not the premise.

There is a preservation argument too, and preparedness-minded people will recognize it. Medical reasoning that lives only inside institutions can shrink or close with them. Held in local AIs, spread across many independent copies, it outlives its institutions the way a seed vault outlives a bad harvest — not a wish for that day, just readiness for it.

One audience among several — and not the first

Plainly: preparedness is one of this project's audiences, not its goal. The first people this serves are those who already have nothing — regions with one doctor for tens of thousands, where no appointment is ever coming and this tool is the only structured medical reasoning within reach. That a consultation costs a few cents of computing is the moral core: keeping it from them is a choice, not a resource problem. Your household's independence and their access come from the same design; by running a copy well, you keep that design alive.

Know what this is not. It is not a doctor, and it is not emergency care. It prepares you for real care; it does not replace it. Where a clinician is reachable, see the clinician. And it will never tell you to medicate yourself — antibiotics and other treatments belong in a clinician's hands, not bought on a guess. Self-medication is one of the documented drivers of drug resistance worldwide.
