Manifesto · For physicians, everywhere

The automation that runs one real practice can run yours.

This project did not begin as an idea. It began as the working documentation system of one practicing physician — and everything that makes it work is published, portable, and free. Not free for a trial. Free the way a method in a journal is free: yours to adopt, adapt, and own.

The part of medicine that burns physicians out

It is rarely the patients. It is the paperwork after the patients: the note, the referral letter, the requisition, the form, the fax, the follow-up message — the second, invisible shift that begins when the clinical day ends. Documentation is the part of medicine that was never the reason anyone became a physician, and it is the part that consumes the most of what is left of them.

The pattern here is simple and it is disciplined. Structured intake comes in, so the story arrives already organized. A panel of more than one independent AI drafts the documents — the note, the reply, the referral — and where the models disagree, the disagreement is shown, not hidden. Then every single item lands in front of you for review. You approve, edit, or dismiss each one. Nothing is sent, filed, or faxed until you say so. The output is ordinary and interoperable: PDFs and faxes that any clinic, pharmacy, or specialist can receive today.

One physician's real experience: the practicing physician this project grew out of saw documentation workload fall on the order of 70% after adopting this pattern. That is one practice's lived result, stated as such — not a promise, and not a benchmark. Your practice will find its own number.

You remain the author of record

The role of the machine here is the role of a scribe or a dictation service, nothing more. It drafts; you decide. Every reviewable action — every message, every document, every fax — passes through your approval before it exists in the world, and you can always choose to handle a case entirely off-tool, in which case the system sends nothing. The panel is built to hard-fail rather than quietly fall back to a single model. Documents arrive unsigned unless you have deliberately configured your own signature; the blank signature line is the default, and the default is the safe one.

Patients who prepare are allies, not competitors

The patient side of this project coaches people to answer the questions a careful clinician would ask, teaches them the warning signs that mean seek care now, and hands them a portable record they own. From the other side of the desk, that is not a threat — it is the patient every physician wishes would walk in: a clear history, a considered question, a cross-checked second opinion they bring to you rather than instead of you. The manifesto calls this an alliance, and it means it in both directions.

And a physician who runs their own node extends further than their own panel. In the places this project puts first — regions where one physician serves tens of thousands — the hours returned by automation are not a convenience. They are consultations that now happen. A physician whose documentation runs itself serves their whole town a little better, and the marginal cost of the machinery is a few cents of computing per case.

Independence, by construction

  • Your own API keys, your own accounts — no shared server, no account with us, because there is no us to have an account with.
  • Runs on your own machine, driven by an AI coding agent reading a published runbook. Built and tested with Claude Code; any capable assistant can follow the same runbook.
  • No vendor and no subscription. Nothing to renew, no one who can raise the price, change the terms, or switch it off.
  • Works in any country's practice context: the inputs are your inbox and your judgment, the outputs are PDFs and faxes — the common tongue of medicine everywhere.
  • Patient-identifying data stays under your control; the runbook's hard rules keep it out of what goes to the AI panel.

Everything is published.

The methods — the panel rules, the review-card pattern, the safety posture — are documented in the open at the published methods. The setup path for a working physician is at physicians — start here. Nothing is held back, because the whole point is that many hands hold it at once.

Your judgment, your jurisdiction. This tool drafts; professional judgment stays yours, and so does professional responsibility. Regulatory and privacy duties differ by country and by specialty — verify your own compliance obligations before putting any patient data through any system. The project publishes one worked example of doing this properly (the HIPAA/Bedrock guide pattern, with a script that checks what can be checked and plain language about the legal steps no script can do for you); treat it as a model to adapt, not a certification.

One practice proved it. Every practice can hold it.
