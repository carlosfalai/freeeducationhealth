# What good output looks like — worked examples for physicians

This page is for a physician who is new to this project and has not
configured anything yet. It shows one fabricated case flowing through the
three artifacts the physician-facing front-end (`instanthpi/`) produces —
a structured recommendation from `core/`, a review card, and a generated
referral PDF — with a short lesson on each: what makes it good, and what
to reject when your own deployment produces something worse.

Every value on this page is a placeholder. `Jane Example`,
`Dr. Example Physician`, `Example Community Clinic`, and `555-0100`-style
numbers are the repo-wide convention for obviously-fake data. No real
patient, real physician, or real conversation appears here, and none may
ever be added — see the hard rules in the root `CLAUDE.md`.

**The fabricated case used throughout:** an adult patient messages the
physician's Spruce inbox reporting a sore throat for 3 days with a mild
fever, no trouble breathing or swallowing, no rash.

## Example 1 — a generated referral PDF

`instanthpi/pdf/generate.cjs` builds a one-page fillable referral letter
from scratch with `pdf-lib`. You can produce the placeholder version
yourself right now, before configuring any credentials:

```bash
cd instanthpi && npm install
node pdf/generate.cjs pdf/sample-referral.pdf
```

Here is the content of a *well-filled* referral for the fabricated case —
suppose the panel and physician agreed Jane Example's recurrent episodes
warrant an ENT opinion:

```
Medical Referral Letter
Generated by instanthpi (freeeducationhealth) -- self-hosted, no shared
infrastructure.

Referring physician:  Dr. Example Physician
Referring clinic:     Example Community Clinic
Patient name:         Jane Example
Date of birth:        1990-01-01
Referred to:          Example ENT Clinic

Reason for referral:
  Recurrent pharyngitis -- 4 episodes in the past 12 months, the current
  episode day 3 with documented fever 38.2C. Assessment for possible
  tonsillectomy candidacy requested.

Clinical summary:
  Otherwise healthy adult. Current episode: sore throat x3 days, Tmax
  38.2C, no dysphagia, no dyspnea, no rash, no drooling. Prior episodes
  resolved with supportive care; one required antibiotics. No known drug
  allergies reported. No red flags identified by intake screening.

Date:                 2026-07-01

_________________________________
Physician signature (sign manually, or configure SIGNATURE_IMAGE_PATH)
```

### Lesson: what makes this referral good

- **Concise.** One page. The receiving clinic needs the question and the
  minimum facts to act on it — not the whole chart. `reasonForReferral`
  is 2–3 sentences ending in an explicit ask ("assessment for possible
  tonsillectomy candidacy requested"), so the recipient knows exactly
  what is being requested of them.
- **Actionable.** The clinical summary carries the specific facts a
  consultant needs to triage the referral (duration, measured temperature,
  pertinent negatives, allergy status) rather than vague language like
  "several recent infections."
- **Correctly structured.** The generator creates real AcroForm fields
  (`fromPhysicianName`, `patientName`, `reasonForReferral`, …), so the
  same letter can be re-filled programmatically later (via `pdf-lib` or
  `instanthpi/pdf/fill.py`) or by hand in any PDF reader. Verify with:
  `python pdf/fill.py list-fields pdf/sample-referral.pdf`.
- **The signature line is blank by default — and that is correct, not a
  bug.** No signature image is ever embedded unless the physician has
  configured their own private, untracked `SIGNATURE_IMAGE_PATH`. Signing
  stays an explicit, physician-controlled step either way. Reject any
  output that arrives pre-signed with a signature you did not configure.
- **Dates are ISO (`2026-07-01`)** — unambiguous across jurisdictions and
  fax machines.

## Example 2 — a Carousel review card

A card is one physician-facing case review unit: what the patient said,
`core/`'s panel recommendation, numbered plan options, and a pre-drafted
reply. Cards are plain JSON files under `instanthpi/carousel/cards/` —
no database. The full field reference is
[`instanthpi/carousel/card-schema.md`](../../instanthpi/carousel/card-schema.md);
this is its worked example (fabricated data, not a real patient):

### Lesson: how to read a card

Read it top-down in this order:

1. **`caseSummary` first.** One or two sentences, written for your eyes
   (never shown to the patient), that let you orient in seconds. Note it
   already carries the pertinent negatives ("no difficulty breathing, no
   rash") — a summary that omits those is a bad summary.
2. **`recommendation.considerations` — deliberately not "diagnoses."**
   These are ranked clinical possibilities. `confidence` (0–1) reflects
   *agreement across the independent panel models*, not certainty of
   diagnosis. Here 0.6 vs 0.3 tells you the panel leaned viral but kept
   strep genuinely on the table — which is why plan option 2 exists.
3. **`divergenceFlag` — the panel's honesty signal.** `false` here means
   the models materially agreed. When it is `true`, the panel members
   disagreed (conflicting red-flag calls, or non-overlapping
   considerations), and the tooling must tell you so prominently rather
   than blend a single confident-sounding answer. A card presented to you
   with `divergenceFlag: true` and no mention of it violates
   `instanthpi/RUNBOOK.md`'s hard rules.
4. **`redFlags`.** Empty means the panel found no urgent findings in the
   intake — it does *not* mean guaranteed benign. When `redFlags` is
   non-empty, two things must be true of a good card: the flags are
   stated prominently, and **plan option 1 is the urgent option** (e.g.
   "Advise seeking care now, given: …"), never buried below routine
   options.
5. **`suggestedNextSteps` — the quality bar for guidance language.** Each
   step is self-contained and assumes the patient may have no doctor
   reachable nearby: concrete self-care first, then the specific,
   observable change that means it is time to travel to care ("breathing
   becomes difficult, drooling develops, or the fever rises above 39.5C
   and stays there beyond 2 days"). A bare "see a doctor if it gets
   worse" fails this bar — reject it and regenerate.
6. **`planOptions` — your decision, pre-structured.** Numbered, each with
   a machine-readable `action` (`spruce_reply`, `spruce_reply_and_pdf`,
   `spruce_reply_and_fax`, `none`). Options should reflect what is
   actually possible for this case (no fax option without a real
   destination number). You choose one; nothing executes until you do.
7. **`draftReply` — yours to edit, never auto-sent.** Notice it is
   phrased for this patient's actual words, not a paste of the JSON
   fields, and it carries the red-flag guidance verbatim into
   patient-facing language. Approving with edits stores your text in
   `decision.editedReply`, which always wins over the draft.
8. **`billingSuggestions` — physician-eyes only.** Here `null` (no
   jurisdiction was configured). When present (see Example 3), these are
   for your own records and are **never shown to the patient** — no
   front-end may surface them patient-side.
9. **Lifecycle:** `pending → approved → sent` (or `dismissed`). `decision`
   and `execution` are separate blocks on purpose — approving a card and
   executing it are two different moments, and both stay on disk for
   audit. A card that claims `sent` with an empty `decision` block should
   never exist; treat it as a bug.

## Example 3 — a structured recommendation object

This is the raw return value of `core/`'s single function,
`getRecommendation(intakeAnswers, config)`, validating against
[`core/schema/recommendation.schema.json`](../../core/schema/recommendation.schema.json).
Same fabricated case, but this time the physician front-end passed
`jurisdiction` in its config, so `billingSuggestions` is populated, and
the optional `panelMeta` diagnostics are included:

```json
{
  "considerations": [
    { "text": "Could be consistent with a viral pharyngitis.", "confidence": 0.6 },
    { "text": "Streptococcal pharyngitis is possible and worth testing for if available.", "confidence": 0.3 },
    { "text": "Infectious mononucleosis is less likely at day 3 without fatigue or lymphadenopathy reported, but worth revisiting if symptoms persist beyond a week.", "confidence": 0.1 }
  ],
  "divergenceFlag": false,
  "redFlags": [],
  "suggestedNextSteps": [
    "Rest, warm salt-water gargles, and acetaminophen or ibuprofen for pain as needed.",
    "Drink fluids regularly even if swallowing is uncomfortable.",
    "If a rapid strep test or throat swab is available nearby, it can help confirm whether antibiotics are needed.",
    "If breathing becomes difficult, drooling develops, or the fever rises above 39.5C and stays there beyond 2 days, seek in-person care as soon as possible.",
    "If a clinic is more than a few hours away and swallowing is getting harder rather than easier, do not wait for it to become severe before starting the journey."
  ],
  "billingSuggestions": [
    {
      "code": "99213",
      "description": "Established patient office/outpatient visit, low complexity (generic CPT-style suggestion for the treating clinician's own records).",
      "jurisdiction": "CPT-generic"
    }
  ],
  "plainLanguageSummary": "This sounds most consistent with a common viral throat infection, which usually improves on its own within a week. A strep test, if one is available near you, can help confirm whether antibiotics would help.",
  "panelMeta": {
    "providersConsulted": ["anthropic", "deepseek", "openai"],
    "panelSize": 3,
    "generatedAt": "2026-07-01T14:01:57.000Z"
  }
}
```

### Lesson: a plain-language walkthrough of each field

- **`considerations`** *(required)* — the ranked list of clinical
  possibilities the panel discussed. Framed as "considerations," never
  "diagnosis," and no front-end may relabel it. Each entry has `text`
  (plain language) and `confidence` (0–1). Read `confidence` as *how
  strongly the independent models agreed*, not as diagnostic certainty —
  three models converging at 0.6 is a different claim than one model
  asserting 0.6. Note the third entry does useful clinical work at
  low confidence: it names what would make it worth revisiting.
- **`divergenceFlag`** *(required)* — `true` when the panel members
  disagreed materially. This flag is the structural substitute for
  physician oversight in deployments where no clinician reviews the
  output, which is why it must be surfaced explicitly, never silently
  resolved by picking one model's answer.
- **`redFlags`** *(required)* — urgent findings from the intake, in plain
  language. An empty array means "the panel identified no urgent
  findings," never "this is guaranteed benign." When non-empty, the
  red-flag guidance must appear inside `suggestedNextSteps` too — a
  recommendation that flags urgency but gives no instruction about it is
  malformed in spirit even if it validates.
- **`suggestedNextSteps`** *(required)* — concrete, ordered,
  self-contained steps calibrated for someone who may have **no doctor
  reachable nearby**. Order matters: least-invasive and
  most-immediately-actionable first, then the specific threshold for
  seeking in-person care, then travel-timing advice for people far from
  any clinic (the last step above exists precisely for that reader).
  This is the field to scrutinize hardest when judging output quality.
- **`billingSuggestions`** *(optional, nullable)* — jurisdiction-aware
  code suggestions for the treating clinician's own records, generated
  only when the front-end passed a `jurisdiction` hint. Physician-facing
  front-ends (`instanthpi/`, `epic/`) may use it; the patient-facing bot
  ignores it; it is **never presented to the patient**. Treat the codes
  as suggestions to verify against your own billing scheme, not as
  billing advice.
- **`plainLanguageSummary`** *(required)* — a short summary suitable for
  direct display to the patient, assuming no medical background and no
  reliable follow-up access to a clinician. In `instanthpi/`, this is raw
  material for the card's `draftReply`, not the reply itself.
- **`panelMeta`** *(optional)* — diagnostics: which providers actually
  responded, how many independent responses were aggregated
  (`panelSize`), and when. Useful for logging in self-hosted
  deployments. If `panelSize` here is ever below the minimum of 2, or
  below what you configured, something upstream broke the contract —
  `getRecommendation()` is required to *throw* when fewer than the
  configured `panelSize` providers succeed, never to quietly downgrade
  to a single-model answer.

## The quality bar, in one checklist

When your own configured deployment starts producing output, hold it to
what you saw above. Reject and regenerate (or fix configuration) if you
see any of these:

- Next steps that reduce to "see a doctor" with no self-care, no specific
  worsening threshold, and no travel guidance for someone far from care.
- Anything labeled a "diagnosis" — the contract says considerations.
- A card presenting a blended, confident answer while
  `divergenceFlag: true` goes unmentioned.
- Non-empty `redFlags` with the urgent option buried below routine plan
  options, or a draft reply that drops the red-flag guidance.
- Billing codes anywhere a patient can see them.
- A single-model answer produced by lowering `panelSize` below 2 to work
  around a failing provider — fix the provider credential instead.
- A generated PDF that arrives pre-signed with a signature image you did
  not configure yourself.
- Any real name, phone number, or health-card number in an example,
  test, or committed file — placeholders (`Jane Example`, `555-0100`)
  only, always.

Everything on this page is reproducible before you configure a single
credential: the PDF via the smoke test in the root `CLAUDE.md`, the card
shape via `instanthpi/carousel/card-schema.md`, and the recommendation
shape via `core/schema/recommendation.schema.json`.
