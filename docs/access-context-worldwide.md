# Worldwide Access Context: Non-Prescription Antibiotics and Self-Pay Diagnostics

**Purpose.** Descriptive public-health / health-policy reference to inform *where* a self-hosted
health-triage tool is most actionable. It maps two access dimensions across regions:
(1) whether people can obtain **antibiotics without a prescription**, and (2) whether people can
**pay for laboratory / diagnostic tests without a physician order**.

**This is not medical advice and must not be used to encourage self-medication.** Non-prescription
antibiotic use is a recognized driver of antimicrobial resistance (AMR); this document treats it as a
*problem to be aware of*, not a pathway to recommend. Nothing here endorses buying specific antibiotics
or bypassing clinical assessment.

**Evidence posture.** Antibiotic-access claims rest on peer-reviewed systematic reviews, simulated-client
(mystery-shopper) pharmacy studies, and WHO/Lancet reports. Self-pay diagnostic-access claims are weaker
and more mixed: much of the international lab-access evidence is commercial or provider-published rather
than peer-reviewed regulation research, and is flagged as such below.

---

## 0. Why this matters: the AMR backdrop

Bacterial AMR was directly responsible for an estimated **1.27 million deaths** and **associated with
4.95 million deaths** worldwide in 2019, making it a leading cause of death globally — higher than
HIV/AIDS or malaria ([GRAM / *The Lancet* 2022](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(21)02724-0/fulltext)).
A later GRAM analysis forecasts AMR-attributable deaths rising toward ~1.9 million/year by 2050
([*The Lancet* 2024](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(24)01867-1/fulltext)).
Easy non-prescription access to antibiotics — enabling self-diagnosis, wrong drug/dose, and short
courses — is one recognized structural driver, and phasing out over-the-counter (OTC) antibiotic sales
is an explicit WHO Global Action Plan objective
([WHO campaign report, PDF](https://cdn.who.int/media/docs/default-source/antimicrobial-resistance/amr-gcp-asa/report_antibiotics-campaign_web.pdf);
[*Lancet Global Health* 2024, "access and prevent excess"](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/langlo/article/PIIS2214-109X(24)00215-8/fulltext)).

## 1. Antibiotics without a prescription

### 1.1 Global picture

A 2023 mixed-methods systematic review (162 studies, 52 countries) found the **pooled prevalence of
non-prescription antibiotic dispensing in community pharmacies was 63.4%** (95% CI 59.6–67.1), higher in
low-income than high-income countries and highest in Sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia/Pacific, and Latin
America/Caribbean — and **not improving over two decades**
([*Lancet Infectious Diseases* 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S1473309923001305);
[CIDRAP summary](https://www.cidrap.umn.edu/study-finds-variation-antibiotic-access-otc-use-asia-and-africa)).
An older but foundational review found non-prescription use accounted for **19–100% of antimicrobial use
outside northern Europe and North America**
([Morgan et al., *Lancet Infect Dis* 2011 / PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3543997/)).
A separate pooled analysis of 38 studies across 24 countries put non-prescription supply at ~62%
([*Antibiotics* 2020, PMC](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7694985/)).

**Three categories used below:**
- **(a) Legal OTC (de jure):** law permits pharmacy sale of at least some systemic antibiotics without a
  prescription. Genuinely rare today; the clearest example is **Thailand**.
- **(b) De jure prescription-only, de facto OTC:** the *dominant* pattern in high-availability regions —
  the law requires a prescription, but pharmacies routinely dispense without one and enforcement is weak.
- **(c) Strict, enforced prescription-only:** law requires a prescription *and* it is largely enforced;
  self-medication rates are low.

> Methodological note: the strongest evidence comes from **simulated-client / standardized-patient**
> studies (a researcher requests an antibiotic and records what is dispensed). These measure *pharmacy
> behaviour*, which is the most policy-relevant metric. Self-report surveys measure *population
> behaviour* and tend to give lower numbers.

### 1.2 Sub-Saharan Africa — very high availability (mostly category b)

Meta-analysis of 23 simulated-client/cross-sectional studies across 9 countries (Ethiopia, Tanzania,
Eritrea, Sudan, Zimbabwe, Kenya, Malawi, Uganda, Zambia, Ghana, Cameroon) found a **pooled
non-prescription dispensing proportion of 69% (95% CI 58–80)**, ranging from 8% (Zimbabwe) to 94%
(Uganda) with high heterogeneity
([*Antibiotics* 2021, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7807893/)).
A Moshi (Tanzania) simulated-client study found **92.3%** of retailers dispensed antibiotics without a
prescription ([PLOS One 2018](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0207465)).
Antibiotics are de jure prescription-only across most of the region; the practical reality is
over-the-counter dispensing by pharmacies and drug shops.

### 1.3 South Asia — high availability with a partial reform (category b, moving toward stricter law)

**India** classifies antibiotics under Schedule H of the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules (prescription
required), and in 2013 added **Schedule H1** for higher-risk agents (newer cephalosporins,
fluoroquinolones, carbapenems, some anti-TB drugs), mandating a dispensing register. Enforcement is the
weak point: with 800,000+ pharmacies and few inspectors, OTC dispensing remains common, and a South
Indian study found **70–90% of pharmacies sold Schedule H1 antibiotics without a prescription**
([Porter et al., *Med Sci Law* 2021](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/09685332211020786);
[Udupi standardized-patient study, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6861094/)).
Simulated-client rates elsewhere in South Asia are also high (e.g., Nepal ~66.5%, Bangladesh ~45.7% of
customers obtaining without prescription per multi-country exit interviews).

### 1.4 East & Southeast Asia — mixed, with one legal-OTC case

- **Thailand — category (a), legal OTC:** antibiotics may lawfully be sold by licensed community
  pharmacists without a physician prescription, unlike neighbours
  ([*Antibiotics* 2021, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7913721/)). (Customer exit-interview
  studies nonetheless report *lower* "without prescription" counts, ~3.9%, reflecting pharmacist
  gatekeeping and how the metric is defined.)
- **Vietnam — category (b), high:** ~55.2% of antibiotic customers obtained them without a prescription,
  and a standardized-patient study found **88–91% of clearly viral URTI presentations were given
  antibiotics** ([*Lancet Regional Health – Western Pacific* 2022](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lanwpc/article/PIIS2666-6065(22)00062-1/fulltext)).
- **Indonesia — category (b), high:** simulated-client dispensing without prescription ~50% (child
  diarrhoea) to ~74% (UTI) ([PMC 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11459306/)).
- **China — category (b)/(c) in transition:** law is prescription-only (only some topical agents are OTC),
  with a G20-era goal of prescription-only enforcement in all provinces by 2020; yet **83.6% of pharmacies
  sold antibiotics without a prescription in 2017**, and sales remained common in 2021 despite tightening
  ([*Bull WHO* 2023, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10140688/);
  [nationwide study, ARIC 2019](https://aricjournal.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13756-019-0655-7);
  [access-excess policy review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8868274/)).

### 1.5 Middle East / North Africa — high availability (category b)

A systematic review of self-medication in the Middle East estimated **~39% of antimicrobials are
purchased without a prescription**, with pharmacy-level complicity documented (e.g., in one Syria study
87% of 200 pharmacies sold antibiotics without a prescription, rising to 97% when the buyer insisted)
([*Int J Clin Pharm* 2017, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5684864/)). Population surveys in
Saudi Arabia report 48–79% having used antibiotics without a prescription
([Saudi review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6035317/)). Egyptian and UAE studies show
similarly high self-medication.

### 1.6 Eastern Europe / former Soviet states — elevated within Europe (category b in practice)

Systemic-antibiotic OTC dispensing occurs in several EU/European states despite being illegal, and
pharmacy purchase without prescription is the **most frequent self-medication pathway in Eastern European
countries**; Southern and Eastern Europe (e.g., Romania, Greece, and non-EU states including Russia)
consistently show higher self-medication and higher resistance than Northern Europe
([Grigoryan et al., *Emerg Infect Dis* 2006](https://wwwnc.cdc.gov/eid/article/12/3/05-0992_article);
[determinants review, *Front Public Health* 2018, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6304439/)).
Weak enforcement (no penalties for pharmacists) is repeatedly cited as the mechanism.

### 1.7 Latin America — historically high, but the region of the clearest reforms

Antibiotics were long dispensed OTC across much of Latin America; the region also has the best-documented
enforcement reforms:
- **Chile (1999):** enforced prescription-only sale; community consumption fell sharply (~12.3 → 8.5
  DID within a year), though the effect eroded after ~2002
  ([Bavestrello et al., *Rev Med Chil* 2002, SciELO](https://www.scielo.cl/scielo.php?script=sci_arttext&pid=S0034-98872002001100009);
  [Chile stewardship overview, *Antibiotics* 2025](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-6382/15/3/247)).
- **Mexico (2010):** the National Health Council enforced the pre-existing (1984) prescription-only law,
  ending routine OTC sale (~40% of pharmacy antibiotic sales had been without prescription)
  ([BMC Public Health 2012, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3562509/);
  [impact study, PLOS One 2013](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3797702/)).
- **Brazil (RDC 44/2010 + SNGPC):** ANVISA required retained prescriptions logged in the National
  Controlled Products Management System (SNGPC); consumption showed an initial spike then decline
  (9.95 DID in 2010 → 8.06 DID in 2012)
  ([Moura et al., *J Glob Antimicrob Resist* 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28735057/);
  [São Paulo analysis, PLOS One](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5152856/)).
- A four-country/19-country analysis found OTC restrictions produced **short-term** consumption drops that
  were often not sustained ([Wirtz et al., *Medicine* 2015](https://journals.lww.com/md-journal/fulltext/2015/09040/the_impact_of_restricting_over_the_counter_sales.30.aspx)).

### 1.8 Strict, enforced prescription-only — Northern Europe, North America, Australasia (category c)

Northern-European systems enforce prescription-only status effectively: reported "last course not obtained
from a health professional" is ~1% (Netherlands) and self-medication ~2–3% (Sweden, Finland), and UK /
German / French / Dutch pharmacies almost never dispense antibiotics without a prescription
([Sweden pathways study, PLOS One 2022](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0273117);
[determinants review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6304439/)). The **US and Canada** are
also enforced prescription-only for systemic antibiotics (leakage occurs mainly via informal channels,
online sellers, and imported/agricultural products rather than at licensed retail counters).

## 2. Self-pay / direct-access diagnostic testing

Two distinct gating models exist worldwide: **physician-order-gated** (a clinician must order the test)
vs **direct-access / self-pay** (a person can order and pay for tests themselves). The evidence base here
is thinner and more commercial than the antibiotic literature; treat market-practice claims as
observational.

### 2.1 United States — legal patchwork, direct-access in a majority of states

"Direct access testing" (DAT) / direct-to-consumer (DTC) testing lets a consumer order lab tests without
a physician order, paying out of pocket. Roughly **37 states plus the District of Columbia permit some or
all direct consumer test ordering**, with **Arizona** the most permissive (virtually any test), while a
few states (e.g., New Hampshire, Rhode Island, and restrictive elements in NY/PA) limit it
([ADLM/AACC "Direct Access Testing"](https://myadlm.org/cln/articles/2020/march/direct-access-testing);
[Direct-to-consumer blood testing, Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct-to-consumer_blood_testing)).
DTC prices are often **below** insurance-negotiated or self-pay physician-ordered prices
([cost-comparison study, PMC 2024](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC11663041/)). Professional
bodies flag interpretation/follow-up risk when results arrive without a clinician
([ADLM position statement](https://myadlm.org/advocacy-and-outreach/position-statements/2019/direct-to-consumer-laboratory-testing)).

### 2.2 United Kingdom — direct-to-consumer alongside a referral-gated NHS

The NHS is physician/GP-order gated, but a parallel **private self-pay** market allows blood tests without
a GP referral (finger-prick home kits or nurse-drawn venous samples), processed in **UKAS-accredited**
labs to the same standards as NHS pathology; Medichecks pioneered UK DTC testing (basic panels ~£60–£120)
([Medichecks "how it works"](https://www.medichecks.com/pages/how-it-works)).

### 2.3 India, Southeast Asia, much of Latin America — routine walk-in private-pay testing (de facto open)

In **India**, private NABL-accredited diagnostic chains and aggregators (e.g., PharmEasy, and many
standalone labs) let anyone book blood tests and full-body panels **without a doctor's prescription**,
including home phlebotomy and pay-at-collection
([PharmEasy Diagnostics](https://pharmeasy.in/diagnostics)). Comparable walk-in, self-pay private-lab
access is common across much of Southeast Asia and Latin America. *Caveat:* this reflects **market
practice** documented largely via commercial/provider sources rather than peer-reviewed regulatory study;
imaging (CT/MRI/X-ray) and some restricted tests more often still require a referral even where routine
blood work does not.

### 2.4 Referral-gated systems (contrast)

Most **public/single-payer** systems gate publicly funded testing behind a clinician order — e.g., NHS
(UK), provincial systems in Canada, and public labs across Europe — where a physician (or authorized
NP/PA where permitted) must order the test for it to be performed/reimbursed
([US CMS lab-order requirements, for the ordering-authority model](https://www.cms.gov/lab-test-order-requirements);
[state ordering-authority guide](https://www.rupahealth.com/post/functional-lab-test-ordering-rights-by-state)).
The pattern globally is that **self-pay private channels are widely available even where the public
channel is gated** — so "gated" usually means *publicly funded* testing, not that self-pay testing is
impossible.

## 4. How this shapes the tool

- **High-self-access regions raise the *actionability* of triage guidance.** Where a person can already
  walk into a pharmacy or private lab, education-level output ("what this pattern of symptoms can mean,
  which red flags mean seek in-person care now, what a clinician would typically check") is more useful
  because the person can actually act on the *seek-care* and *get-evaluated* steps. The tool's value is
  highest exactly where formal clinician access is scarce and informal access is easy.
- **But easy access is precisely why the tool must NOT prescribe.** These same regions carry the heaviest
  AMR burden *because* antibiotics are easy to obtain. Telling a user which antibiotic to buy would push
  the exact behaviour the AMR literature identifies as harmful. The tool therefore stays at
  **education / triage / red-flag** altitude and never names a specific antibiotic to purchase, a dose, or
  a "just go get X" instruction.
- **This matches the project's existing stance** ("not a doctor; considerations, not prescriptions").
  Direct-access *testing* guidance is lower-risk than drug guidance and can be framed as "these are the
  kinds of tests a clinician would consider for this picture" — informational, not a substitute for a
  physician's order or interpretation — while **drug guidance stays strictly non-directive**.
- **Practical framing per region:** in enforced-Rx-only regions (Northern Europe, North America), the
  natural next step the tool points to is "see a clinician / GP" because that is the only legal path to
  both antibiotics and (publicly funded) testing. In high-access regions, the tool can additionally
  acknowledge that self-pay evaluation is realistic — while still routing antibiotic decisions to a
  clinician and emphasizing red flags and AMR/safety.

## 5. Evidence-quality caveats

- **Antibiotic access** is well-supported: multiple systematic reviews and simulated-client studies
  converge on the regional pattern above. Country-level numbers carry wide confidence intervals and high
  heterogeneity (e.g., SSA 8–94%), so treat single-country figures as indicative, not precise.
- **Self-pay diagnostic access** is **less rigorously documented**. US direct-access state counts and the
  UK model rest on professional-body and provider sources; India/Latin America/Southeast Asia "open"
  status is drawn largely from commercial lab practice rather than peer-reviewed regulatory analysis, and
  varies by *test type* (routine blood work is widely self-pay; imaging and restricted assays more often
  still require a referral). Where a claim is market-practice rather than statute, it is labelled above.
- **Reforms are not permanent.** Chile and Brazil show that enforced restrictions can cut consumption
  quickly but that effects may erode without sustained enforcement — so "enforced prescription-only" is a
  live policy state, not a fixed country attribute.
